### What MiroFish is

Think of it as a crowd simulator for information shocks.

You feed it a seed — a headline, a document, a policy draft, a report, a leak, a rumor — plus a question.  
It spins up a synthetic society: agents with memory, incentives, and different ways of interpreting the same input.

It’s already public. Anyone can run it.
The edge is being early enough to actually use it.

They don’t just reply to you.  
They interact, form factions, amplify narratives, and converge on a story — the same way real crowds do.

What you get back isn’t a prophecy.  
It’s a map of competing narratives, who pushes them, who believes them, and what the crowd is likely to do next.

### What it outputs (so you can audit it)

**1) A report** you can skim like an analyst note.  
What narratives formed, which one took over, and what the crowd does next once it believes it.

**2) A graph** — the map of the story.  
Who matters, what connects to what, which clusters are forming.

**3) Interviews.**  
Pick an agent and ask:
- What do you think happened?
- What would change your mind?
- What do you do next — and why?

You don’t just get a conclusion.  
You get the crowd’s reasoning — and you can poke it until it breaks.

### The belief machine (one line)

seed → interpretations → factions → amplification → commitment → action

It always starts with a seed. One thing happens (or looks like it happened).

Then interpretations split: “nothing burger” vs “we’re cooked.”  
Factions form around interpretations because stories are how humans coordinate.

Amplification is when the story starts copying itself:  
people share it because other people share it.

Commitment is the switch: the crowd stops debating and starts moving.  
“Just in case” becomes “do it now.”

Action is what makes it real: selling, freezing, boycotting, piling on, pulling out.  
And once action begins, it produces new “evidence” (screenshots, price moves, delays) that feeds the story again.

### The 5‑minute simulation playbook (without fooling yourself)

1) **Pick a seed with uncertainty + stakes.**  
Ambiguity is fuel. Zero ambiguity = no interpretation. Zero grounding = hallucinations.

2) **Define the frame in one sentence.**  
“Simulate the next 48 hours of public reaction.”  
Or: “Simulate how employees + customers + investors react over the next week.”

3) **Write the requirement like you’re briefing a sharp analyst.**  
“Identify the top 3 narratives, the 2 factions that dominate early, the turning point, and the next actions.”

4) **Treat the run like a lab experiment.**  
You’re not looking for “the answer.” You’re looking for the *shape*: how fast it polarizes, what story dominates, and why.

5) **Use the graph to find the hidden center of gravity.**  
Most debates pretend to be about “the event,” but they orbit a few nodes: motive, responsibility, fairness, safety, competence.

6) **Interview agents like you’re cross‑examining a witness.**  
Ask: “What would change your mind?” “What are you reacting to?” “What do you do next if you’re wrong?”

7) **Inject one minimal credible change.**  
Clarification, denial, missing datapoint. If the dominant story collapses instantly, you learned something: it was mostly coordination + emotion.

### The unstable extreme checklist (fast)

If you’re ticking 6+ boxes, you’re watching a crowd in a mode.

**Velocity**
- Speed beats sources → stop debating facts; you’re watching coordination.
- Confidence rises while clarity doesn’t → label a mode shift, not “truth discovery.”
- Conditional language disappears (“maybe” → “confirmed”) → ask: “What would make this false?”
- Reaction becomes the evidence → separate seed from feedback loop.

**Narrative**
- Phrase monoculture → write down the 1–2 dominant narratives.
- Complexity collapses into one clean story → look for the suppressed competing story.
- A villain becomes mandatory → expect escalation.
- Turning points are emotional, not evidential → track the artifact that flipped the room.

**Coordination**
- “Just in case” stampede → expect second-order artifacts next.
- Micro‑artifacts spawn (clips, screenshots, charts) → ask which drive action vs decorate.
- A few hubs steer the room → shifting hubs can flip the room.
- Costs stop mattering → that’s where overreactions are born.

### Where this gets real: prediction markets

Prediction markets are the cleanest place to see this because they put a number on belief.

They turn “the crowd thinks X will happen” into a price.  
And once belief has a price, reversals stop being philosophical. They become trades.

That’s the whole game: separating evidence from the panic premium.

Now here’s where MiroFish gets unfair:
you’re not guessing the crowd’s probability — you’re simulating why the crowd is at that probability, and whether it’s stable or just panic.

**A real Polymarket example**

On July 3, 2024, a New York Times report suggested Biden was weighing his future in the race.
Polymarket reacted instantly — “Biden drops out” spiked toward ~80%.

A White House spokesperson then said the NYT story was false.

This is where crowds compress time:
“Maybe later” gets traded as “any minute now.”

Polymarket even had a short‑dated market: “Biden drops out by Friday?” (July 5, 2024).
It resolved No.

Now the MiroFish move:
don’t try to predict Biden.
Predict the crowd.

Feed it the seed (NYT report + the denial + the timing constraints) and ask:
- What narratives form in the first hours?
- Which narrative wins by default?
- Does the “imminent drop‑out” story survive a minimal credible challenge, or does it collapse?

If the simulated crowd can’t hold the “by Friday” narrative without leaning on pure momentum (“everyone is saying it”), that’s not evidence-backed belief.
That’s a panic regime.

And panic regimes are exactly where “reverse the crowd” becomes a trade.

### The Polymarket reversal playbook (with MiroFish)

1) Prefer deadline markets. Crowds are worst at time.
2) Write the crowd’s story in one sentence.  
3) Simulate the next 24–72 hours — not the whole universe.  
4) Interrogate the deadline: “Why by this date?” “What has to happen first?” “What slows it down?”  
5) Trade the unwind, not the prophecy.

**Another real Polymarket example: “Fed emergency rate cut in 2024?”**

In early August 2024, fear hit markets and traders reached for the oldest panic story in finance:
“The Fed will step in. Emergency cut. Any minute now.”

Polymarket even had a market for it — and at one point the implied probability jumped to 50%.  
But the contract resolved No. No emergency cut happened in that window.

This is the kind of trade MiroFish is built for:
not “will the Fed cut?” but “is this deadline belief structurally supported — or just the bailout reflex spreading faster than evidence?”
